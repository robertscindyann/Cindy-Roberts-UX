Case Study: Diabetes Awareness for Teens

For my passion project, I designed an educational website aimed at high school students to raise awareness about type 2 diabetes. Since more than 5,700 kids and teens (ages 10–19) are diagnosed each year, I wanted to create a platform that makes this complex topic easier to understand and encourages healthier choices.

The Problem

Diabetes information online is often:

  • Overly medical → hard for teens to relate to
  • Text-heavy → not engaging for a younger audience
  • Adult-focused → most resources target parents or healthcare providers

High school students need simple, engaging, and relatable content that connects to their everyday life (sports, snacks, energy levels).

Research & Insights

  • I reviewed CDC and ADA (American Diabetes Association) resources to ensure medical accuracy.
  • I spoke informally with teens and parents to understand what motivates teens to care about health.
  • Key insight: visuals, interactivity, and clear “what this means for me” messaging are crucial for engagement.

User Interviews

I interviewed high school students to better understand their awareness of diabetes. The findings revealed important knowledge gaps and shaped how I designed the site.

Knowledge Gap

Most students didn’t know what the pancreas does or how the body uses food for energy.

Insulin Awareness

Students had heard of insulin but couldn’t explain its role clearly.

Education Need

They believed diabetes basics should be taught in high school health classes.

Relevance Perception

They thought diabetes mainly affects overweight people and didn’t see themselves at risk.

Design Goals

  • Simplify language → use a conversational tone, avoid jargon.
  • Visual-first learning → diagrams, icons, and images to explain concepts.
  • Relatability → highlight activities and foods teens recognize.
  • Actionable choices → show how lifestyle changes (sports, superfoods) connect to prevention.
  • Ensure accessibility with high-contrast colors, alt text, keyboard navigation, and plain language.

Design Process

Information Architecture (Hub-and-Spoke)

I structured the website using a hub-and-spoke model. The homepage acts as the hub, giving a clear overview, while subpages (spokes) allow users to dive deeper into focused topics:

  • What is Diabetes? — Simple introduction with plain language.
  • How the Body Works — Visuals showing the pancreas and insulin process.
  • Food → Energy — Relatable examples using everyday foods.
  • Effects on Daily Life — Icons illustrating symptoms like thirst and fatigue.
  • Prevention Tips — Actionable steps, such as exercise and superfoods.

This approach ensures teens can explore topics at their own pace without feeling overwhelmed.

Wireframing & Layout

  • I started with low-fidelity sketches to plan the hierarchy of content.
  • Focused on short sections + strong visuals so users wouldn’t feel overwhelmed.

Visual Design

  • Colors: Used bold, high-contrast colors for accessibility and energy.
  • Typography: Chose Roboto for a clean, modern look that reads easily on screens.
  • Imagery: Real teens in action (sports, classroom, daily life) + icons to reinforce key ideas.

Accessibility Considerations

  • Readable fonts: Chose Roboto with generous line spacing for easy scanning.
  • Color contrast: Used high-contrast colors to ensure text is legible against backgrounds (helpful for color-blind users).
  • Alt text: Added descriptive alt tags to all images and icons so screen readers can convey meaning.
  • Skip navigation link: Included a “Skip to main content” link for users navigating with keyboards.
  • Video content: Embedded an educational video but ensured text explanations were also present for users who can’t access audio.
  • Plain language: Used simple, conversational text to reduce cognitive load, making it easier for both teens and users with reading difficulties.

Prototype & Development

  • Interactive mockups created in Figma.
  • Final site built with HTML5, CSS3, and responsive design for all devices.

The Solution

A responsive educational website that helps teens:

  • Understand what diabetes is (without medical jargon).
  • See how it affects the body through visuals and icons.
  • Learn prevention tips through relatable actions (basketball, choosing healthy foods).
  • Stay engaged with a mix of text, images, and video content.

Reflection & Next Steps

  • What worked: The balance of visuals + short copy made the content approachable for a younger audience.
  • What I’d improve: Add interactive elements like quizzes (“Is this a superfood?”), gamified challenges (track your weekly activity), or peer stories.
  • Impact potential: With more testing in schools or health clubs, this could become a tool for early diabetes prevention education.
