Designing an Educational Experience for Health Awareness

UX Case Study — Diabetes Information Website (Designed & Coded)

  • Role: UX Designer & Front-End Developer
  • Timeline: 6 weeks
  • Tools: Figma, HTML, CSS, JavaScript
  • Deliverables: Research synthesis, IA (hub-and-spoke), wireframes, responsive build

I designed and coded a responsive educational site to make diabetes basics easier to understand. The experience focuses on plain language, calm visuals, and clear navigation so new learners can scan, orient, and dive deeper without cognitive overload.

The Problem

Health content online can be dense, medicalized, and hard to navigate—especially for new learners.

  • Pages often group too much information without hierarchy.
  • Terminology creates friction for readers with lower health literacy.
  • Navigation does not support quick orientation or progressive exploration.

Research & Insights

  • Reviewed CDC and American Diabetes Association resources to ensure medical accuracy.
  • Conducted semi-structured interviews with 3 high school students (ages 15–18) to understand baseline knowledge and perceptions of diabetes.
  • Used ChatGPT to refine interview questions and assist with thematic analysis of responses.
  • Key insight: teens need visuals, interactivity, and clear “what this means for me” messaging to stay engaged.

User Interviews

I asked students 11 open-ended questions about diabetes. Their responses revealed consistent knowledge gaps that directly informed design decisions.

Knowledge Gap

Students confused diabetes with high blood pressure and circulation problems. → Led to a simplified “How the Body Works” section with clear visuals of the pancreas and insulin process.

Insulin Awareness

They had heard of insulin but didn’t understand its role. → Informed creation of an animated diagram showing how insulin regulates blood sugar.

Education Need

All students believed diabetes basics should be taught in school. → Website content was written in a conversational, classroom-friendly tone.

Relevance Perception

Students thought diabetes mainly affects overweight people and didn’t see themselves at risk. → Design emphasized prevention and daily relevance for all teens.

Design Goals

  • Simplify language – conversational tone, no jargon.
  • Visual-first learning – diagrams, icons, and animations to explain key concepts.
  • Relatable content – examples from sports, school, and common foods.
  • Actionable choices – connect lifestyle activities (exercise, diet) with prevention.
  • Accessibility – high-contrast colors, alt text, plain language, and keyboard navigation.

Design Process

Information Architecture (Hub-and-Spoke)

I used a hub-and-spoke model. The homepage acts as the hub with clear navigation, while subpages (spokes) allow users to dive deeper into focused topics:

  • What is Diabetes? – Simple introduction with plain language.
  • How the Body Works – Visuals showing the pancreas and insulin.
  • Food → Energy – Everyday food examples made relatable.
  • Effects on Daily Life – Icons for symptoms like thirst and fatigue.
  • Prevention Tips – Actionable advice like exercise and healthy snacks.

This approach ensures teens can explore topics at their own pace without feeling overwhelmed.

Wireframing & Layout

  • Started with low-fidelity sketches to map content hierarchy.
  • Prioritized short sections with strong visuals to avoid overwhelming readers.

Visual Design

  • Colors: Bold, high-contrast palette for accessibility and energy.
  • Typography: Clean sans-serif font for easy reading on screens.
  • Imagery: Teens in relatable contexts (sports, school) + icons for clarity.

Accessibility Considerations

  • Readable typography with generous line spacing.
  • High-contrast colors for legibility, supporting color-blind users.
  • Alt text on all images and icons for screen readers.
  • “Skip to main content” link for keyboard navigation.
  • Embedded video paired with text for multiple learning modes.
  • Plain language throughout to reduce cognitive load.

Prototype & Development

  • Interactive mockups created in Figma.
  • Built final site with HTML5, CSS3, and responsive design for mobile, tablet, and desktop.

Front-End Build

I hand-coded the site using semantic HTML and modern CSS. Light JavaScript supports small interactions (e.g., mobile menu toggle) while keeping the experience fast and distraction-free.

  • Responsive grid + fluid type scale for comfortable reading on any device.
  • Semantic landmarks (header, nav, main, footer) and accessible link styling.
  • Consistent spacing system and component tokens for maintainability.

The Solution

A responsive educational website that helps teens:

  • Understand diabetes basics without medical jargon.
  • See how the body and insulin work through simple visuals.
  • Learn prevention tips connected to relatable activities.
  • Stay engaged with a mix of text, icons, and video content.

Impact

  • Students reported the site format felt more “approachable” than what they had seen before.
  • Validated need for diabetes education in high school health classes.
  • Demonstrated how AI can accelerate research analysis and insight generation.

Reflection & Next Steps

Building this project strengthened my skills in structuring complex information and coding for readability. If I iterate, I’ll add simple wayfinding aids (breadcrumb, “On this page” anchor list) and gather usability feedback from first-time learners to validate clarity.
